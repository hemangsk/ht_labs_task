India and Alcohol Use Disorders

How alcoholism relates to crime and gender based violence, and how two indian states have mastered the art of moderate-controlled drinking plan.

A ccording to the report of World Health Organisation, one in twenty Indian males have an addiction or alcohol related disorder.

The harmful psychological effects of uncontrolled alcohol consumption leads to serious threats to the society. Alcoholism, also known as Alcohol Use Disorders is quite evidently the 'most economical mechanism' to cause chaos in the Indian society.


The story assumes that density of alcoholism in an area is directly proportional to number of people drinking alcohol.

For a overview of the chaos and disorder created, we can look at the following data provided by Ministry of Road Transport and Highways which shows the number of people affected by uncontrolled alcohol consumption, and how important it is to have a controlled drinking plan.




In the graph of Alcohol Consumption v/s Gender based violence, an observation is evident that Sikkim has the second highest percentage of population consuming alcohol which is nearly double the National Average of 15.6 %. Also, on the other hand, it can be observed that it has the lowest percentage of victims of spousal violence (1%).

Following a similar pattern is Arunachal Pradesh in alcohol consumption, which is at 43%, thrice the National Average. Also, the percentage of victims of spousal violence in Arunachal Pradesh is less than the National Average of 15.14%.

Infact, a few more inference about Sikkim and Arunachal Pradesh are evident from the visualization below:

  • The percentage of women having their own Bank Account and/or Saving Account in Arunachal Pradesh and Sikkim, is greater than the national average of 51.83 %.

  • Sikkim has the third largest percentage population of women having a mobile phone that they themselves use.

  • At 59.7% Arunachal Pradesh has the third largest percentage population of women having a bank or savings account that they themselves use.

  • Only exception is in Sikkim where percentage women who own a house or land in their name is 17% less than the National Average of 38.9%.



Sikkim and Arunachal Pradesh have been comparatively better in fight against the social evil of gender inequality than the other parts of India as the graph shows. Essentially one can say they have been able to provide both the genders equal opportunties and ensured that they deserve to enjoy same rights, relative to other states. A story on report by Centre for Strategic and International Studies concluded Sikkim has the best working conditions for women, with absolute zero restrictions on women working in night shifts at retail establishments, factories and IT Sector.


At 86.6 % percent of Women and 91.5 % percent of Men who are literate, Sikkim is well above the National Average of 76.69 % (for Women) and 84.99 % (for Men). From abandoning social stigmas to breaking the taboos, Sikkim has set up a nice example, being backed by a good literacy rate.

As an example, the trend in disparity in alcohol consumption between men and women throughout India is very huge except in Arunanchal Pradesh and Sikkim. On the contrary, this can't be said about other states of India where alcohol consumption patterns of women and men are completely different. Why is it so?

One cant simply claim that a higher alcohol consumption rate even among women exemplifies women empowerment, it merely states a variation in alcohol consuming choices. However, we can say that there is a better attitude pertaining to alcohol consumption in this part of India, consumption of alcohol without being disapproved or frowned upon and the fact that women can make the choice without having to deal with the typical thou-shalt-not mindset prevalent in a lot of other states.

Essentially, Sikkim (and Arunachal Pradesh also for the most part) have been able to master the art of moderate-controlled alcohol consumption, curbing the gender based violence and alcohol induced crimes, wuth support of a well above average literacy rate and equal opportunities throughout genders.
